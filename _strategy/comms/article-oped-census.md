[HOOK — Version A, census release week:]
This week, Canadians learned how many of us there are, and where. The first results of the 2026 Census will ripple outward for months: cities recalculating transit routes, school boards projecting enrolment, businesses deciding where to open, and every level of government adjusting the formulas that move billions of dollars. Most of us will glance at a headline about growth and move on.

We should pause a moment longer, because the machine that produced those numbers is more fragile than it looks. We know, because we broke it once.

[END HOOK]

In 2010, the federal government replaced the mandatory long-form census with a voluntary survey. The reasoning sounded harmless — why compel people to answer questions? The 2011 results answered that question the hard way. Response rates collapsed from roughly 94 per cent to under 70, and the people who stopped responding were not a random sample: lower-income households, Indigenous communities, recent immigrants and rural residents went missing from the national portrait in disproportionate numbers. Data for many smaller communities was withheld entirely because it could no longer be trusted. The mandatory long form returned in 2016, and Canadians — to their credit — answered it at record rates. But 2011 remains a permanent hole in the country's statistical record. Anyone tracing a trend through that year must route around it, forever. When data collection weakens, the gap never heals.

I think about that episode the way an engineer thinks about a bridge that cracked but didn't fall. Not as a partisan story — the lesson is not about one government — but as a demonstration that the quality of what we know about ourselves is a choice, made continuously, and reversible in either direction.

It is an easy choice to get wrong, because good statistics are invisible in the way all good infrastructure is invisible. Nobody thanks the water main. The census, the Labour Force Survey, the consumer price index — these feel like natural features of the landscape, numbers that would exist whether or not anyone worked at them. They would not. Behind each is an unglamorous apparatus of questionnaire design, sampling theory, quality control and confidentiality protection, most of it done by Statistics Canada, an agency respected worldwide and rarely thought about at home.

And the pressures on that apparatus are growing, not shrinking. Statistical agencies everywhere face the same headwinds: survey response rates have been falling for decades across the Western world, as fewer people answer the phone or the knock at the door. Budgets tighten, and surveys are quiet, easy things to cut — the damage shows up years later, in decisions that were a little worse than they needed to be, with no press release to mark it. Meanwhile the demand side grows: in an era when anyone can generate a confident-looking chart, and misinformation is cheap, an authoritative common source of facts is worth more than ever, not less.

That is the part each of us can actually do something about. A statistical system is sustained by two acts of citizenship. The first is answering: the census, and the voluntary surveys where declining response quietly erodes quality every year. The second, less obvious, is using the data. Public numbers that are downloaded, cited, taught and argued over are politically expensive to cut. Numbers nobody looks at are not. I have spent the past two years on my own small contribution to the using side — an independent, free website that gathers Canada's public data into charts anyone can check and cite — and the deeper I went, the more impressed I became with what our public agencies publish openly, and the more convinced that this openness is an asset we hold too casually.

The numbers in this week's headlines will shape decisions about your neighbourhood for the next five years. They exist because thousands of people did careful, tedious work, and because millions of Canadians filled in a form. That bargain — we all answer honestly, and in return we all get to know the truth about our country — is one of the quieter achievements of Canadian public life.

We have already run the experiment on what happens when we take it for granted. Once was enough.

*Dan Weaver is an Associate Professor, Teaching Stream, at the University of Toronto Scarborough and the creator of Canada Observatory (URL), an independent, non-partisan public data site.*
