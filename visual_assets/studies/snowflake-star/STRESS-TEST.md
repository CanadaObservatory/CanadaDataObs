# Stress test — the 12-point star against its harshest critic

Adversarial review, 2026-06-19. Written *against* the design (the brief was "stress
test against potential criticism"), then balanced into a verdict. Evidence rendered to
`_stress/` (`scale-evidence.svg.png`, `coherence.svg.png`) — judged at true pixels, the
way the leaf and seal were.

The lead under test: **`star12-leaf-navy`** (12-point, leaf-ray-locked, six area
colours, off-white skeleton), with its solid/outline/seal siblings.

---

## The criticisms, strongest first

### 1. It throws away the leaf's single best asset: instant "Canada" (fatal-class)
The botanical leaf's whole reason for winning (DESIGN-CRITERIA criterion 10) is that a
first-time visitor reads **"Canada" in under a second**. **A star does not.** Stars are
the most nationally *promiscuous* glyph there is — dozens of flags, agencies, parties,
and brands use them. At the sizes where this mark survives (see §3) it reads as *a
star*, not *Canada*. We would be trading a globally-legible national signifier for a
generic one. Nothing in the mark says "Canada" except, faintly, the palette.

### 2. The "felt connection" to the leaf is felt by us, not the audience
The derivation is genuinely elegant — but it is only legible **if you have seen the
morph**. A viewer meeting `star12-leaf-navy` cold does not think "maple leaf"; they
think compass / snowflake / pinwheel. The structural honesty (short points on the leaf
rays) is **designer-internal satisfaction**, invisible at the point of contact. So the
mark cannot inherit the leaf's equity by lineage — it has to earn recognition on its
own, and on its own it says nothing specific.

### 3. It does not survive small — and "fixing" that reopens a closed problem (empirical)
`scale-evidence.svg.png`, true pixels magnified:
- **Coloured (`leaf-navy`, `leaf-seal`): muddy below ~32px.** At 16px it is a navy/maroon
  smudge with a pale centre; the six colours fog into noise and the white points vanish.
  This is the *exact* failure (sub-pixel detail → fog) that demoted the botanical leaf
  and forced the seal. A colour star would need its own simplified small companion —
  **reintroducing the two-cut problem the brand explicitly killed** ("one display leaf +
  ONE SEAL", Addendum 2).
- **Solid (`solid-navy`): survives, but only as a generic spiky star** — an asterisk /
  sparkle / sea-urchin. It holds a shape at 16–24px but holds *no distinctiveness and no
  Canada* there, which is precisely where identity is formed most often (tabs, avatars).
- **Outline: display-only.** Thin strokes fog below ~48px, same as the existing
  outline-leaf family.

So: the legible-small version is generic; the distinctive version isn't legible small.

### 4. It reopens the dilution the brand fought to close (empirical, fatal-class)
`coherence.svg.png`: the system would then hold **four marks — leaf, seal, north star,
and this**. Two are stars (this vs `mark-northstar`); two are discs (the seal vs this).
The brand's hard-won resolution was *reduction* — "one leaf, one seal," after explicitly
naming "two competing marks" as the vulnerability that retired the classic leaf. **This
adds a fourth mark and a second star.** Either it *replaces* the north star or it
shouldn't exist; as an addition it is strictly dilutive.

### 5. The crowded-glyph / ownability problem (criterion 3)
Compass rose, mariner's star, sheriff/police star, ship's wheel, sparkle/loading-spinner,
mandala, kaleidoscope, snowflake — this lands in one of the most saturated symbol spaces
in design. It would be **hard to own and hard to protect**, the opposite of the leaf's
"furthest from every other maple mark" distinctiveness.

### 6. Register risk: it can read off-mission (criterion 8 authority, criterion 9 independence)
- **Coloured radial → pinwheel / fidget-spinner / beachball / cruise-line / wellness-
  chakra / casino compass.** Playful, touristy, or new-age registers — wrong for an
  authoritative, citable data institution.
- **Star-in-a-disc → official seal / military or police badge / insignia.** That pushes
  *toward* government/officialdom — the very thing the flag-leaf test was rejected for
  (it read as the RCAF roundel). A compass-star-in-disc risks reading as an agency seal,
  undermining the visible non-partisan independence.
- **Snowflake → holiday/Christmas cliché, ski-resort, "special snowflake" pejorative,
  Frozen.** And "winter" is a reductive stand-in for a country that is not only winter.

### 7. The original brief quietly evaporated
The premise was a **symbolic point count** — 10 provinces / 11 / 13 federation. We ended
at **12, which encodes nothing civic** ("6 rays + 6 bisectors" is an internal process,
not a meaning a viewer reads). If the symbolism was the point, 12 fails the brief; we
kept the *construction* and dropped the *reason*. A critic asks: "why twelve?" and the
honest answer is "geometry," not "Canada."

### 8. Craft nitpicks that compound
- **Irregularity reads as error, not intent.** The leaf-derived asymmetry (uneven gaps)
  can look like a *slightly broken* star at a glance — especially small — rather than
  deliberate organic geometry. The intent is invisible.
- **Colour-on-matching-ground fragility.** The navy point on navy (and maroon on maroon)
  only works because we added an off-white outline — a patch that signals a structural
  weakness and breaks in one-colour, on alternate grounds, and in print.
- **Twelve colours meeting at a hubless centre** can mud at small sizes now that the
  pivot dot is gone.
- **Reproduction (criteria 2, 12):** thin facets + separators degrade in embroidery,
  stamp, fax, 1-colour, low-DPI — many small parts, the opposite of robust.

primary mark or the tiny mark** — full stop.

## What survives the stress test

One position, and only one: **a display-only SECONDARY motif that replaces (not joins)
`mark-northstar`.** As a richer footer/section/About-page device used at **≥48px**, the
fatal criticisms soften:
- it never has to carry Canada-recognition alone (the leaf/seal/wordmark do that);
- it never renders at favicon/avatar size (so §3 is moot);
- the leaf lineage can be *shown* where it lives (an About "Our marks" page with the
  morph + `star12-proof`), converting §2 from a weakness into the mark's story.

For that to be real it must: **(a) replace the north star, not add to it** (kills §4);
**(b) demonstrably read as kin to the leaf** rather than competing with it — test
`leaf-navy` beside the cells leaf in the actual footer; **(c)** lead with **solid-navy or
the seal** for any sub-48px reduction, accepting it becomes a plain star there; and
**(d)** the owner must accept that "12 / observatory / snowflake" is **decorative-
symbolic**, not a federation count.

## Verdict

- **As the logo / favicon / navbar / avatar:** rejected — fails scale (§3) and Canada-
  recognition (§1, §10).
- **As an additive fourth mark:** rejected — dilutive (§4).
- **As a display-only replacement for the north star**, leaf-shown via an explainer:
  **viable but optional** — a nice-to-have secondary motif, not a brand need. The honest
  framing is that this solves an *aesthetic puzzle we set ourselves*, not a *problem the
  identity has*. If it ships, it ships as the north star's successor and nothing more.

**Single biggest unresolved question for the owner:** is there any job here that the
existing `mark-northstar` doesn't already do? If not, the disciplined answer the brand's
own criteria would give is *don't add it* — keep the win of "one leaf, one seal, one
quiet star."
